Monitor Stand Riser questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Monitor stand risers improve ergonomics by lifting your screen to eye level, freeing desk space, and often adding storage. The right choice depends on your monitor size and weight, desired height, and whether you need extras like a laptop tray or cable management. Most users benefit from a riser that adjusts to their natural line of sight while sitting or standing.

What does a monitor stand riser actually do?

A monitor stand riser is a platform that elevates your screen so the top bezel sits roughly at eye level when you’re seated. This simple change can dramatically reduce forward head posture and neck strain, which many desk workers experience from hunching over a low screen. By lifting the display, you also reclaim desk real estate underneath the riser, turning wasted space into storage for your keyboard, notebook, or a small docking station. Beyond ergonomics, a riser can improve airflow around your monitor and make your workspace look more organized. Some models include built-in drawers, cable routing channels, or even a secondary shelf for a laptop. While they don’t replace an adjustable monitor arm for full positioning freedom, they offer a stable, easy-to-install solution that works with almost any desk.

How do I choose the right height and size?

The ideal height is one that puts the top quarter of your screen at or just below your natural horizontal line of sight when sitting upright. For most people, that means a riser that adds 4 to 6 inches of elevation, though taller users or those on higher chairs may need more. Measure from your desk surface to your seated eye level, subtract the height of your monitor’s base, and look for a riser that bridges that gap. Width and depth matter just as much. Your riser should be wide enough to support the monitor base without overhang and deep enough that the screen sits securely without tipping. A riser that’s too narrow can become unstable with a large monitor, while one that’s too shallow might leave the base hanging off the front edge. Most fixed-height risers come in widths between 20 and 36 inches, but adjustable models allow you to tweak the height and sometimes the tilt angle for fine-tuning.

Will my monitor fit? Weight and base considerations

Every riser has a weight limit, usually listed by the manufacturer. Typical limits range from 15 pounds for small risers to 50 pounds or more for heavy-duty models built for ultra-wide or dual setups. Weigh your monitor (including its stand) and choose a riser that can handle at least a few extra pounds as a safety margin. Don’t forget the footprint of the base: a monitor with a very wide or deep V-shaped base may not sit flat on a narrow riser. If you’re using a monitor arm, you generally won’t need a riser at all, the arm provides height adjustment. But some people combine a low-profile riser with an arm to gain storage underneath while keeping the screen height adjustable. For curved or unusually shaped monitors, a riser with a flat, solid top rather than a slatted or divided surface offers the most stable platform.

What materials and construction should I look for?

Monitor risers are commonly made from bamboo, solid wood, metal, tempered glass, or high-density plastic. Bamboo and solid wood are popular for their warm look and durability, but they can be heavy, which adds stability. Metal risers, often steel or aluminum, are very strong and can be built with a more open, industrial aesthetic; they’re a good choice for heavy monitors. Tempered glass risers look sleek and are easy to clean, but they can be slippery and may chip if bumped. Plastic risers are lightweight and budget-friendly but may flex under heavier loads. Regardless of material, check that the riser has non-slip pads on the top and bottom to prevent your monitor from sliding and to protect your desk surface. For standing desk users, a lighter but sturdy riser (like aluminum) is easier to reposition when switching between sit and stand.

Can I use a monitor riser on a standing desk?

Yes, a riser works on a sit-stand desk, but it adds both height and weight to the desk top. This can slightly reduce your desk’s overall standing height range, especially if your desk already has a limited maximum height. Taller users may find that a riser plus a desk at full height pushes the screen too high; if you’re over 6 feet tall, test the combination or look for a desk with an extended height range. Stability is also a concern. A tall riser on a standing desk can wobble more when the desk is raised, especially if the riser isn’t very wide or the desk itself isn’t rock-solid. For standing setups, a shorter, wider riser with a low center of gravity is usually safer. Some people prefer to skip the riser entirely and rely on the desk’s own height adjustment, but a small riser can still be useful for tilting or adding a second screen.

What about cable management and extra features?

Many modern risers come with built-in cable grommets, channels, or clips to route monitor, keyboard, and charging cables underneath, reducing visual clutter. Some include a sliding drawer for storing a keyboard, mouse, or small accessories when not in use. A few premium models integrate a USB hub or wireless charging pad, though these features often add significant cost. If your workspace is tight, consider a riser with a laptop shelf or a tiered design that lets you stack a laptop beneath the monitor while keeping both screens at usable heights. Just be mindful that stacked setups can trap heat, so choose a riser with an open or ventilated structure if you’re using a laptop underneath. Ultimately, extra features are nice but shouldn’t compromise the riser’s primary job of holding your monitor safely and at the right height.

Frequently asked questions

Does a monitor riser work with a curved monitor?

Yes, as long as the riser’s top surface is flat and wide enough to accommodate the monitor’s base. Curved screens don’t need special risers because the base is the same as a flat monitor of similar size.

Can I use a monitor riser with a laptop?

Absolutely. A riser lifts a laptop screen to a more comfortable viewing angle, and many models have a dedicated laptop stand shelf. Just make sure the riser is stable enough to support the laptop’s weight and that ventilation isn’t blocked.

Is a monitor riser the same as a monitor arm?

No. A riser is a passive platform that sits on your desk, while a monitor arm clamps to the desk edge and attaches to the monitor’s VESA mount. Arms offer full adjustability in height, tilt, and distance; risers are simpler, cheaper, and don’t require drilling or VESA compatibility.

How much weight can a typical monitor riser hold?

Standard risers support 15 to 30 pounds, enough for most single monitors up to about 32 inches. Heavy-duty models can hold 40 to 50 pounds, which works for larger ultrawide or dual-monitor setups. Always check the manufacturer’s rating before buying.

Do I need a riser if I already have a standing desk?

Not necessarily, but it can help if you want to fine-tune your eye level or add storage underneath. Keep in mind that a riser will raise your minimum and maximum desk heights, which could be a problem for very tall or short users.

How do I clean a monitor riser without damaging it?

For wood or bamboo risers, use a slightly damp microfiber cloth and dry immediately to avoid water damage. Glass or metal risers can be cleaned with a standard glass cleaner or mild soap. Avoid abrasive pads or strong solvents, especially on painted or laminated surfaces.

In shortChoosing a monitor stand riser comes down to matching your screen’s size and weight with a stable platform at the right height. Measure your desk and your seated eye level, decide what extra features actually matter to you, and don’t sacrifice solid construction for a low price. A well-chosen riser is a simple, affordable upgrade that can make your workday noticeably more comfortable.
