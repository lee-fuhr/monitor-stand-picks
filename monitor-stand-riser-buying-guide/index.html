How to choose the right monitor stand riser

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A monitor stand riser lifts your screen to eye level, reducing neck strain and reclaiming desk space. Look for a height between 4 and 8 inches, a wide enough base to support your monitor’s feet, and a sturdy build from metal or thick bamboo. Prioritize a riser that fits your monitor’s VESA pattern if you want to use an arm, or a simple shelf if you just need to raise the factory stand.

What does a monitor stand riser actually do?

A monitor stand riser – sometimes called a desk shelf, monitor platform, or laptop riser – lifts your screen so the top bezel sits at or near eye level. This simple change aligns your neck and spine, which helps prevent the dreaded “tech neck” after long hours at the desk. Many risers also free up space underneath for a keyboard, notebook, or secondary devices, turning a cluttered desk into a more efficient workspace. Beyond ergonomics, risers can improve airflow around your laptop or monitor, keeping components cooler. Some models include built-in cable management channels or storage drawers, though those extras often add bulk and cost. The core job remains the same: get the screen higher without wobble.

What height should my monitor riser be?

Most ergonomic guidelines recommend that your monitor’s top bezel be roughly at eye level when you’re seated with your shoulders relaxed. For an average desk height of 29 inches and an average seated eye height of 44 to 48 inches, a riser that adds 4 to 8 inches of lift works for most people. Taller individuals or those who prefer a more upright sitting posture may need a riser on the higher end of that range. The best approach is to measure your current setup: sit naturally, close your eyes, then open them and note where your gaze lands. If it’s below the top edge of the screen, you need a riser. If your monitor has a very low stand, a riser can compensate; if you already use a tall arm, you might not need a separate riser at all. Adjustable-height risers let you dial in the perfect level, but fixed risers are simpler and often sturdier.

Fixed shelf vs. adjustable arm: which is better for you?

Fixed shelf risers are simple, solid platforms that sit on your desk. They’re typically made of wood, metal, or heavy plastic. They offer stability, no moving parts, and often include a lower shelf for storing your keyboard when not in use. The downside: you can’t change the height or angle after purchase, so you have to pick the right one from the start. Adjustable monitor arms, by contrast, are gas-spring or mechanical arms that clamp to the desk and hold the monitor in the air. They give you infinite height, tilt, and swivel adjustment. But they often cost more (in comparison), require VESA compatibility, and can wobble if not properly installed. For someone who switches between sitting and standing throughout the day, an adjustable arm is hard to beat. For a fixed-height desk where you just need a simple lift, a shelf riser is usually the more practical and affordable choice.

How do I know if my monitor is compatible with a riser?

Compatibility has two parts: the riser’s size and your monitor’s base. First, check the width of your monitor’s feet. Most risers are designed for screens up to about 32 inches, but ultrawide monitors or heavy gaming displays may need a wider platform or a reinforced surface. Measure the distance between the legs or the footprint of the base and match it to the riser’s usable surface area. Second, if you’re considering a monitor arm riser, your monitor must have VESA mounting holes (typically 75x75mm or 100x100mm) on the back. Most modern monitors do, but some budget or very slim models omit them. If your monitor doesn’t have VESA, you’re limited to shelf risers or adhesive brackets that may not be as secure. Always check the monitor’s specs or look at the back panel before buying.

Are there any downsides to using a monitor riser?

The most common complaint is that a riser adds vertical height to the desk, which can push a monitor too high for shorter users or those in a low-seated position. That’s why adjustable-height models are worth considering if multiple people share the desk. Another issue: some risers, especially tall ones, can introduce slight wobble if the desk isn’t sturdy or if you frequently type with force. Heavy metal risers are usually the most stable. A less obvious downside is that a riser can interfere with your desk’s aesthetics or cable routing. Large risers can block access to USB ports on the back of a monitor, and they can create a visual barrier. If you use a laptop with the lid closed, a riser might also make it harder to access the power button or ports. Test your workflow mentally before committing.

What materials and build quality should I prioritize?

Solid wood, thick bamboo, and heavy-gauge steel are the gold standards. They provide the heft needed to resist wobbling and distribute weight evenly. Pressed wood or particleboard can work for lightweight monitors but may break down over time. Plastic risers are generally less stable and can flex, though high-density ABS can be decent in a pinch. Look for rubber or silicone pads on the top surface to prevent scratching your monitor’s base and on the bottom to keep the riser from sliding around. A non-slip surface is especially important if you keep a laptop on the riser. Cable management – like a cutout or a clip underneath – is a nice touch, but don’t sacrifice overall stability for it. A well-built riser that feels solid when you touch it is almost always a better long-term buy.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a monitor riser with a standing desk?

Yes, but it’s often unnecessary because standing desks are already adjustable in height. A riser can still help if you want to create a dual-level surface (e.g., keep your laptop at eye level while using a separate keyboard) or if your standing desk doesn’t rise high enough for your tall frame.

How much weight can a typical monitor riser hold?

Most standard monitor risers are rated to hold between 20 and 50 pounds. That covers the vast majority of monitors up to about 32 inches. Heavier ultrawides or all-in-one desktops may exceed that, so always check the weight capacity before buying, and err on the side of a stronger riser.

Do I need a VESA mount to use a monitor riser?

No. A shelf riser doesn’t attach to the monitor at all – it simply sits under the base. VESA is only needed for arm-style risers that hold the monitor from the rear. If you’re just looking to raise the factory stand, a shelf riser is all you need.

Will a monitor riser help with neck pain?

For many people, yes, if the pain is caused by looking down at a low screen. Raising the monitor to eye level eases the strain on the cervical spine. However, neck pain can also result from poor seating posture or glare, so the riser is one piece of a larger ergonomic puzzle.

Can I place a laptop on a monitor riser instead of a monitor?

Absolutely. Many people use a riser to elevate their laptop to eye level while using an external keyboard and mouse. Just make sure the riser has enough surface area to support the laptop’s feet and that it allows for airflow underneath to prevent overheating.

Is there a difference between a monitor riser and a laptop stand?

Yes. A monitor riser is typically larger and made to support a monitor’s wide base, while a laptop stand is narrower and often foldable or angled for ventilation. You can use a monitor riser for a laptop, but a laptop-specific stand may offer better airflow and a tilt angle for typing if you don’t use an external keyboard.

In shortThe best monitor stand riser for you comes down to your screen size, desk space, and preferred ergonomic style. A simple, sturdy shelf riser is often the most cost-effective and stable solution for most desks, while an adjustable arm gives you the most flexibility if you switch between sitting and standing. Measure your monitor’s base, check for VESA compatibility if that’s your path, and pick a material that feels solid under your hands. Your neck and shoulders will thank you.
